  • Taj Mahal Palace

    Mumbai, India

    Opened December 16, 1903 and built by Jamsetji Tata, the Taj was conceived as a “hotel worthy of Bombay” and quickly became India’s flagship luxury property. Today its Indo-Saracenic façade, grand public rooms and harbourfront location beside the Gateway of India make it a historic icon that blends Raj-era grandeur with modern hospitality.

  • Raffles Hotel

    Singapore

    Founded by the Sarkies brothers in 1887, Raffles began as a modest seaside hotel and grew into a colonial-era landmark famous for its long verandas, elegant suites and the birthplace of the Singapore Sling cocktail. It remains a symbol of Singapore’s heritage and refined service.

  • The Plaza

    New York City, USA.

    Opened October 1, 1907, the Plaza is a landmark French-Renaissance–style hotel on Central Park South that has hosted dignitaries, film premieres and high-society events for more than a century. Its opulent public rooms, ballroom and historic address make it one of Manhattan’s most recognizable luxury hotels

  • Four Seasons Resort Bora Bora

    French Polynesia

    Opened in 2008, this overwater-bungalow resort sits on Motu Tehotu and is renowned for its lagoon sanctuaries, coral conservation programs and spectacular water villas — a honeymoon and luxury-resort icon in the Pacific. The property combines Polynesian aesthetics with Four Seasons service standards.

  • COMO Maalifushi

    Maldives

    Opened 2014, Maalifushi is COMO’s Maldives private-island resort on the pristine Thaa Atoll; it’s praised for diving, understated villa design and COMO’s wellness/food philosophy. The resort is a modern favorite for families and divers seeking a quieter Maldivian atoll.

  • Amanpuri

    Phuket, Thailand (Aman’s original resort)

    Launched in 1988 as Aman Resorts’ flagship, Amanpuri’s minimalist, pavilions-and-villas concept (designed by Ed Tuttle) set a new standard in discreet, location-sensitive luxury and inspired many later resorts. Its private beach, simple Thai-inspired architecture and emphasis on serenity helped catalyze the modern “luxury retreat” movement.

  • Villa d’Este

    Lake Como, Italy

    Dating back to 1568 as a Renaissance summer palace, Villa d’Este evolved into a luxury hotel famous for its terraced gardens, fountains and lakeside grandeur — a symbol of aristocratic retreats on Lake Como. Its long artistic history and manicured grounds attract guests seeking stately elegance.

  • Villa Ephrussi de Rothschild

    Saint-Jean-Cap-Ferrat, France

    Built for Béatrice Ephrussi in the early 1900s (completed 1912), this flamboyant villa and its nine themed gardens showcase Rothschild art collections and Belle Époque luxury on the French Riviera. It remains a celebrated historic villa open to visitors for tours and special events.

  • Aman-style private villas (example: Amanjena villas)

    Marrakech, Morocco

    Amanjena (opened 2000) and its villa residences were designed by Ed Tuttle in a Moorish/Andalusian style; the property’s private pavilions and villas are model examples of contemporary luxury villas inspired by local tradition and crafted for privacy. Aman-brand villas are noted for design coherence, seclusion and bespoke service.

  • Blue Swallow Motel

    Tucumcari, New Mexico, USA.

    Open since 1939 on historic Route 66, Blue Swallow is a restored neon-lit motor lodge that preserves mid-century charm — individual cottages, vintage signage and family ownership make it a classic roadside stop. It’s listed in travel guides as a quintessential Route 66 experience

  • Wigwam Motel (Holbrook)

    Holbrook, Arizona, USA.

    Built in 1950, the Wigwam Village features concrete “wigwam” cabins (actually tipi-shaped) and is one of the surviving themed motels from America’s golden road-trip era; it’s on the National Register of Historic Places. Its quirky, photogenic units capture mid-century Americana and remain popular with Route 66 travelers.

  • Madonna Inn

    San Luis Obispo, California, USA.

    Opened in 1958, the Madonna Inn grew from 12 themed rooms into a flamboyantly decorated landmark known for individually designed suites, a pink dining room and eccentric décor — a beloved roadside destination on California’s Central Coast. Visitors come for the novelty rooms, bakery and memorable photo ops.
